Sustainable Floral Design for Your Home

Learn how to create beautiful, eco-friendly floral arrangements that minimize waste and maximize beauty with tips from our expert florists.

By Emily Chen Lead Florist
A sustainable arrangement featuring seasonal peonies and reusable containers

At Bridgeland Market Gifts, we believe beauty and sustainability can go hand in hand. Traditional floral arrangements often create significant waste, but with a few thoughtful choices, you can enjoy stunning flowers while minimizing your environmental impact. Here's our comprehensive guide to sustainable floral design for your home.

Why Sustainable Florals Matter

The floral industry has a larger environmental footprint than many people realize. Consider these facts:

78%
of cut flowers are imported, creating significant carbon emissions
90%
of floral foam ends up in landfills where it never biodegrades
60%
more pesticides are used on flowers than on food crops

By making conscious choices about the flowers we buy and how we arrange them, we can significantly reduce these impacts while still enjoying nature's beauty.

Choosing Sustainable Flowers

The foundation of any sustainable arrangement is the flowers themselves. Here's what to look for:

Seasonal Selections

Choose flowers that are in season in your area. Seasonal blooms don't require energy-intensive greenhouses or long-distance transportation. In Calgary, our seasonal favorites include:

  • Spring: Tulips, daffodils, peonies
  • Summer: Sunflowers, zinnias, dahlias
  • Fall: Chrysanthemums, asters, ornamental grasses
  • Winter: Evergreen branches, holly, amaryllis

Locally Grown

Support local flower farmers who use sustainable growing practices. We're proud to source from Alberta growers like:

  • Prairie Gardens (Bon Accord)
  • Blue Mountain Biodynamic Farms (Bragg Creek)
  • Green Valley Flowers (Okotoks)

Local flowers are typically fresher, last longer, and have a much smaller carbon footprint than imported blooms.

Organic & Chemical-Free

Look for flowers grown without synthetic pesticides or fertilizers. These are better for the environment, pollinators, and your home's air quality. Ask your florist about their growers' practices.

Sustainable Design Techniques

Once you've selected your flowers, consider these eco-friendly design approaches:

1. Ditch the Floral Foam

Traditional floral foam is a petroleum-based product that creates microplastic pollution. Try these alternatives instead:

  • Chicken wire: Creates structure in vases and lasts for years
  • Reusable flower frogs: Vintage or modern pin holders
  • Natural mechanics: Use crisscrossed branches or curly willow
  • Water tubes: For individual stems when needed

2. Choose Reusable Containers

Instead of single-use vases or plastic, consider:

  • Vintage bottles or jars
  • Ceramic vessels from local artisans
  • Repurposed household items (teacups, tins, etc.)
  • Biodegradable options like hollowed-out vegetables for special occasions

We offer a container return program where you can bring back our vases for reuse.

3. Incorporate Dried Elements

Extend the life of your arrangements by including dried flowers and foliage. Many blooms dry beautifully:

  • Lavender
  • Strawflowers
  • Eucalyptus
  • Pampas grass
  • Hydrangeas

You can also dry your fresh flowers when they begin to fade by hanging them upside down in a dark, dry place.

Step-by-Step: Sustainable Centerpiece

Let's put these principles into practice with a simple seasonal centerpiece:

1

Select Your Vessel

Choose a reusable container like a ceramic bowl or wide-mouthed jar. For this example, we're using a vintage mixing bowl from a thrift store.

2

Create a Grid

Instead of foam, make a grid across the top of your container using waterproof tape or flexible branches. This will help hold stems in place.

3

Add Foliage First

Start with locally foraged greenery like maple branches or evergreen sprigs to create your structure.

4

Arrange Seasonal Blooms

Add your seasonal flowers, working from largest to smallest. For summer, this might mean sunflowers first, then zinnias, finishing with smaller filler flowers.

5

Include Dried Elements

Tuck in some dried lavender or wheat stalks for texture and longevity.

Caring for Your Sustainable Arrangement

To maximize the lifespan of your flowers:

  • Change water every 2 days
  • Trim stems at an angle every few days
  • Keep away from direct sunlight and fruit (which emits ethylene gas)
  • Remove wilting flowers to keep others fresh longer
