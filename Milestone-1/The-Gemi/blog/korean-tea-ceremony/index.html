The Art of Korean Tea Ceremonies

How centuries-old traditions influence modern Korean café culture

Written by

Soo-ah Park

Co-founder & Pastry Chef at Gami Caffè

Traditional darye ceremony at a Seoul tea house (Photo: Korea Tourism Organization)

Korean tea ceremonies, known as darye (다례), are more than just preparing and drinking tea—they're a meditative practice that embodies Korean philosophy, aesthetics, and hospitality. At Gami Caffè, we draw inspiration from these traditions in our service and tea preparations.

The History of Korean Tea Culture

Tea was first introduced to Korea during the Three Kingdoms period (57 BC–668 AD) by Buddhist monks returning from China. By the Goryeo Dynasty (918–1392), tea culture had flourished, particularly in Buddhist temples. The Joseon Dynasty saw tea ceremonies become more formalized, though tea consumption declined among the general population until its modern revival.

Traditional Korean tea utensils from the Joseon period

Types of Korean Tea Ceremonies

There are several forms of Korean tea ceremonies, each with distinct purposes and settings:

1. Royal Darye

Performed for royalty, these elaborate ceremonies could last hours and included multiple courses of tea paired with traditional sweets.

2. Buddhist Tea Ceremony

Practiced in temples, these ceremonies emphasize mindfulness and meditation, often performed as part of spiritual practice.

3. Everyday Darye

The simplified version practiced in homes and modern tea houses, focusing on hospitality and connection.

"The Korean tea ceremony isn't about perfection of form, but about perfecting the heart. The irregularities in handmade tea bowls remind us of nature's beauty."

- Master Tea Ceremonist Lee Kwang-ho

Key Elements of a Traditional Ceremony

Tea Selection

Korean green teas like ujeon (first flush) and sejak (second flush) are most common, along with herbal infusions.

Utensils

Includes dawan (tea bowl), jeollok (water heater), and dasik (tea sweets).

Movements

Precise, graceful motions for preparing and serving, always with both hands.

Modern Adaptations in Korean Cafés

While traditional tea houses still exist throughout Korea, modern cafés have adapted elements of tea ceremony culture:

  • Presentation: Many cafés serve tea in traditional bowls with careful attention to aesthetics
  • Service Style: The respectful, attentive service in Korean cafés stems from tea ceremony etiquette
  • Pairings: Traditional tea sweets have inspired modern Korean desserts like our injeolmi cake
  • Atmosphere: The tranquil mood of tea ceremonies influences café design and music choices

Health Benefits of Korean Teas

Beyond cultural significance, Korean teas offer numerous health benefits:

Tea Type Key Benefits Caffeine Level
Green Tea (Nokcha) High in antioxidants, boosts metabolism Medium
Barley Tea (Boricha) Aids digestion, caffeine-free None
Citron Tea (Yujacha) High in vitamin C, immune support None
Persimmon Tea (Sukhacha) Rich in tannins, good for circulation None

Try at Home: Simple Korean Tea Ritual

You'll Need:

  • 2 tsp Korean green tea leaves
  • 300ml water at 70°C
  • Traditional tea bowl or wide cup
  • Small sweets (optional)

Steps:

  1. Warm your tea vessel with hot water, then discard
  2. Add tea leaves to the vessel
  3. Pour water and steep for 2 minutes
  4. Enjoy in three mindful sips, appreciating aroma and flavor
  5. Pair with a light sweet between sips
