New Findings Reveal WA Leptospermum Honey Potency Exceeds Manuka

April 3, 2025 10 min read
University of Western Australia Research Team

Groundbreaking research from the University of Western Australia has confirmed that honey derived from native Leptospermum species in Western Australia's Wheatbelt region exhibits antibacterial activity up to four times more potent than New Zealand's famous Manuka honey, positioning WA as a potential global leader in medical-grade honey production.

The Research Breakthrough

A three-year study conducted by UWA's School of Agriculture and Environment, in collaboration with Bee Central, analyzed the chemical composition and antibacterial properties of honey produced from 12 different Leptospermum species native to Western Australia. The research focused on measuring levels of Methylglyoxal (MGO), the compound responsible for Manuka honey's renowned antibacterial effects.

Key findings from the peer-reviewed study, published in the Journal of Agricultural and Food Chemistry, include:

  • Exceptional potency: Several WA Leptospermum species produced honey with MGO levels exceeding 1,500 mg/kg, compared to typical Manuka honey at 300-700 mg/kg
  • Unique chemical markers: Identification of three previously undocumented bioactive compounds contributing to enhanced antibacterial effects
  • Geographical advantage: Honey from the Wheatbelt region showed consistently higher activity than samples from other parts of Australia
  • Seasonal stability: Potency remained high across different flowering seasons, unlike more variable Manuka production

Scientific Significance

"These findings fundamentally change our understanding of active honey potential in Australia," said lead researcher Dr. Emily Chen. "Western Australia's unique biodiversity and environmental conditions create a perfect storm for producing what may be the world's most therapeutically valuable honey."

Comparing WA Active Honey to Manuka

New Zealand's Manuka honey industry has grown from NZ$75 million in 2010 to over NZ$1 billion today, driven by global demand for its medicinal properties. The UWA research suggests WA could develop an even more valuable industry based on these new findings.

"Our medicinal honey is as good as, or more active than, New Zealand medicinal honey," said Dr. Peter Brooks, a senior chemistry lecturer at USC's Honey Lab who collaborated on the research. "New Zealand's got one species [Leptospermum scoparium], we've got multiple species, and some of our species are three or four times more active than New Zealand's."

Implications for WA Agriculture

The research validates Bee Central's farm plantation partnership model, which establishes dedicated Leptospermum plantations on Wheatbelt farms to produce premium active honey. With scientific proof of WA's competitive advantage, the economic potential for regional farmers is substantial.

Economic Impact

At current Manuka prices (A$150-300/kg for medical-grade), a mature WA active honey industry could generate $750 million annually, transforming the economics of marginal farming land.

Environmental Benefits

Leptospermum plantations improve soil health, support biodiversity, and sequester carbon while requiring minimal water compared to traditional crops.

Wayne Collins of Tamma Hill Farm in York, one of Bee Central's plantation partners, noted: "In this day and age, it's nice to diversify. It's a natural product that we find unique and would be good for our environment. The biggest benefits from the partnership is with the bees—they are a critical part of our farming operation."

Medical Applications

The enhanced potency of WA active honey opens new possibilities for medical applications beyond those currently served by Manuka honey:

Wound Care

Potential for advanced wound dressings to treat chronic ulcers and burns

Respiratory

Inhalation therapies for cystic fibrosis and resistant pneumonia

Drug Delivery

Novel drug delivery systems leveraging honey's natural properties

Professor Mark Ashton, a microbiologist at UWA not involved in the study, commented: "The levels of non-peroxide activity we're seeing in these WA honey samples are extraordinary. This could revolutionize how we approach antibiotic-resistant infections in clinical settings."

The Bee Central Research Initiative

Bee Central has committed $2.5 million over five years to establish the Australian Active Honey Research Program at UWA, which will focus on:

  • Genetic mapping of high-potency Leptospermum varieties
  • Optimizing plantation management for maximum bioactive yield
  • Clinical trials for specific medical applications
  • Developing standardized testing protocols for the Australian industry

The program will be based at the planned Bee Shed discovery center in York, creating a hub for research, education, and industry collaboration.

Next Steps for the Industry

With scientific validation of WA's competitive advantage, attention now turns to scaling production and establishing international market recognition. Key priorities include:

  1. Industry standards: Developing an Australian Active Honey certification system
  2. Market development: Educating international buyers about WA honey's superior potency
  3. Supply chain investment: Building specialized processing facilities in the Wheatbelt
  4. Genetic resource protection: Safeguarding WA's unique Leptospermum genetics

Bee Central is currently accepting expressions of interest from Wheatbelt farmers interested in establishing Leptospermum plantations. With proper site selection and management, these plantations can begin producing commercial quantities of nectar within four years.
