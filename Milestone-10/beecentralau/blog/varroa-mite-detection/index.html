Varroa Mite Detection Confirmed in Victoria: Implications for Australia's Bee Industry

June 22, 2025 8 min read

The Australian honey bee industry faces one of its most significant biosecurity challenges with the confirmed detection of Varroa destructor mites in Victoria. This development has triggered emergency response protocols and raised concerns about the potential impact on Australia's agricultural sector, which relies heavily on honey bee pollination.

The Discovery and Immediate Response

On June 22, 2025, routine surveillance by the Agriculture Victoria Apiary Compliance team identified Varroa mites in a single hive located at a property in Nangiloc, near Mildura. The detection occurred through standard sticky mat testing, which revealed a very low level of mite infestation. This marks the first established detection of Varroa destructor in Australia outside of previous incursions that were successfully eradicated.

Within hours of confirmation, Agriculture Victoria activated its emergency response plan, implementing several critical measures:

  • Establishment of a 10km surveillance zone around the infected premise
  • Immediate restrictions on movement of bees, hives, and equipment within the zone
  • Comprehensive inspection of all apiaries in the affected region
  • Trace forward and trace back investigations to determine the origin and potential spread

Beekeeper Action Required

All beekeepers, particularly those in Victoria and neighboring states, should:

  • Monitor hives closely for signs of Varroa mites
  • Review and enhance biosecurity measures
  • Report any suspicious findings immediately to the Exotic Plant Pest Hotline: 1800 084 881
  • Register all hives with the relevant state authority

Understanding the Varroa Threat

Varroa destructor is a parasitic mite that attacks honey bees (Apis mellifera), feeding on their fat bodies and transmitting deadly viruses. These tiny external parasites have devastated bee populations worldwide and are considered the most serious global threat to managed honey bees.

The mites reproduce in brood cells, with females laying eggs on developing bee larvae. Emerging bees often show deformities, particularly deformed wing virus, which significantly reduces their lifespan and ability to function within the colony. Uncontrolled infestations typically lead to colony collapse within 2-3 years.

Economic Impact

Australia's agricultural sector depends on honey bees for pollination of crops worth $14.2 billion annually, including almonds, apples, avocados, and many other fruits and vegetables.

Global Perspective

Since its spread from Asia in the mid-20th century, Varroa has contributed to significant honey bee declines in North America (45% loss since 1987), Europe (25% loss since 1985), and New Zealand (30-40% loss since 2000).

Western Australia's Unique Position

Western Australia's strict quarantine measures and geographic isolation provide some protection against the immediate spread of Varroa mites from the eastern states. However, the detection in Victoria serves as a critical reminder of the need for enhanced vigilance and biosecurity preparedness.

Bee Central is working closely with the Department of Primary Industries and Regional Development (DPIRD) to:

  • Monitor the situation in Victoria and assess potential risks to WA
  • Review and strengthen existing biosecurity protocols
  • Educate beekeepers about early detection methods
  • Prepare contingency plans should Varroa reach Western Australia

WA's current Varroa-free status provides a significant advantage for our honey exports, particularly for premium products like active Leptospermum honey. Maintaining this status requires collective vigilance from all industry participants.

Detection and Identification

Early detection remains our best defense against Varroa establishment. Beekeepers should regularly inspect hives for these key indicators:

Sign Description
Adult bees with mites Reddish-brown oval mites (1-1.8mm) visible on bees' bodies, often between abdominal segments
Deformed wings Newly emerged bees with crumpled, stunted, or missing wings
Brood abnormalities Discolored, sunken or perforated cappings; dead or dying brood
Colony decline Rapid population loss despite adequate food stores

For accurate detection, beekeepers should implement regular monitoring using at least one of these methods:

  1. Sugar shake test: Shaking bees in a jar with powdered sugar to dislodge mites
  2. Alcohol wash: Submerging bees in alcohol to kill and dislodge mites
  3. Sticky board: Placing a greased board beneath the hive to catch falling mites
  4. Drone brood inspection: Examining drone brood cells where mites prefer to reproduce

Looking Ahead: Research and Preparedness

While the current detection represents a serious challenge, Australia has several advantages in responding to this threat:

  • Early detection: The infestation appears limited to a small area
  • Previous experience: Australia successfully eradicated Varroa in 2016 and 2019
  • Research capacity: Strong scientific expertise in bee health and biosecurity

Bee Central is committed to supporting research efforts through our partnership with the University of Western Australia. Current research priorities include:

  • Development of rapid diagnostic tools for field detection
  • Evaluation of treatment options suitable for Australian conditions
  • Breeding programs for Varroa-resistant bee stocks
  • Biosecurity protocols for commercial beekeeping operations

What You Can Do

All Australians can contribute to protecting our honey bees:

For Beekeepers

  • Register all hives with your state authority
  • Implement regular monitoring protocols
  • Maintain detailed records of hive movements
  • Attend biosecurity training workshops

For the Public

  • Support local beekeepers by buying Australian honey
  • Plant bee-friendly gardens with native flora
  • Avoid using pesticides harmful to pollinators
  • Report feral bee colonies to local authorities

Bee Central will continue to monitor the situation closely and provide updates as new information becomes available. Together, we can work to protect Australia's vital honey bee populations and the agricultural industries that depend on them.
