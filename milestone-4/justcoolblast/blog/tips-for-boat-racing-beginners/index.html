Behind the Scenes: Creating Boat Attack

Published on April 30, 2025 • 7 min read • By JustCool Team

Today we're pulling back the curtain to share the development journey of Boat Attack - from early prototypes to the polished game you can play today. This post will give you insight into our creative process, technical challenges, and the lessons we learned along the way.

The Beginning: Concept Phase

Boat Attack started as a simple idea during a team brainstorming session in early 2025. We wanted to create a racing game that felt different from typical car racing titles. After considering various vehicles, we settled on boats because:

  • Water physics present unique gameplay challenges
  • Mobile games lacked high-quality boat racing experiences
  • The visual possibilities with water and environments were exciting

Development Timeline

February 2025 - Prototyping

Our first prototype was built in Unity with basic boat models and placeholder water. The core mechanics we tested included:

// Early boat movement pseudocode
void Update() {
  float forwardSpeed = Input.GetAxis("Vertical") * speed;
  float turnSpeed = Input.GetAxis("Horizontal") * rotationSpeed;
  ApplyBuoyancy();
  ApplyWaterDrag();
}

April 2025 - Water Physics Breakthrough

After struggling with unrealistic boat movement, we developed a custom water physics solution that:

  • Calculated buoyancy based on mesh vertices
  • Simulated wave interactions
  • Optimized for mobile performance
"The water physics system took 3 months longer than planned, but it became our game's standout feature." - Lead Developer

August 2025 - Art Direction Finalized

We experimented with several visual styles before settling on our vibrant, semi-realistic look. Key decisions included:

  • Stylized water shaders with realistic lighting
  • Optimized environment assets that work on mobile
  • Dynamic weather system for visual variety

Technical Challenges

Mobile Performance Optimization

Getting the game to run smoothly across a range of devices required:

  • Custom LOD (Level of Detail) systems for water and environments
  • Efficient use of Unity's SRP (Scriptable Render Pipeline)
  • Aggressive texture compression without quality loss

Lessons Learned

Looking back, here are our key takeaways from developing Boat Attack:

  • Prototype early: Our initial prototypes saved months of development by identifying fun mechanics quickly
  • Mobile first: Designing for mobile constraints from day one prevented major reworks
  • Community feedback: Our beta testers provided invaluable input that shaped the final game
  • Scope carefully: We had to cut some ambitious features to ship a polished product

What's Next for Our Team?

We're currently working on new content for Boat Attack while also prototyping our next game. The lessons from this project are directly influencing our future work, particularly in:

  • More advanced physics systems
  • Improved multiplayer infrastructure
  • Procedural content generation techniques
