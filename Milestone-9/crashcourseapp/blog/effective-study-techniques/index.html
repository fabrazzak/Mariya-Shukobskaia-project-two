5 Effective Study Techniques Backed by Science

Research-proven methods to enhance your learning and retention using the Crash Course app.

July 5, 2025 • 5 min read

In today's fast-paced educational environment, students need study methods that deliver maximum results with minimum wasted effort. Cognitive scientists have spent decades researching how we learn best, and their findings can revolutionize your study sessions with the Crash Course app.

1. Spaced Repetition: The Key to Long-Term Retention

Spaced repetition is arguably the most powerful study technique identified by learning science. This method involves reviewing information at gradually increasing intervals, which has been shown to dramatically improve long-term retention compared to cramming.

The Crash Course app implements spaced repetition through its flashcard system. When you mark a flashcard as "easy," the app will schedule it for review at a later date than if you mark it as "hard." This algorithm, based on the forgetting curve discovered by psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus, ensures you review material just as you're about to forget it.

Research published in the journal Psychological Science found that students using spaced repetition retained information for years, while those using traditional study methods forgot most material within weeks. To maximize this technique:

  • Use the app's flashcards daily, even if just for 10-15 minutes
  • Don't skip scheduled review sessions
  • Be honest when rating your confidence with each card
  • Combine with other subjects for better time management

2. Active Recall: Testing Yourself to Strengthen Memory

Active recall is the practice of actively trying to remember information without looking at the source. A 2013 study in Science showed that students who practiced active recall performed significantly better than those who simply re-read material.

The Crash Course app facilitates active recall through its quiz features. Before watching a video, try to recall what you know about the topic. After watching, use the app's quizzes to test your understanding without referring back to the video.

"Active recall creates stronger memory traces than passive review because it forces your brain to reconstruct knowledge, making future retrieval easier." - Dr. Henry Roediger, Memory Researcher

For even better results, create your own questions based on video content and test yourself later. This dual-layer of active recall (app quizzes plus your own questions) creates robust neural pathways for the information.

3. Interleaving: Mixing Subjects for Deeper Learning

Interleaving involves mixing different topics or subjects during study sessions, rather than focusing on one topic at a time (blocking). A University of South Florida study found interleaving improved test performance by 25% compared to blocked study.

With Crash Course's diverse content library, you can easily practice interleaving:

  • Alternate between science and humanities videos
  • Mix flashcard decks from different subjects
  • Create study sessions combining chemistry and anatomy concepts

This technique works because it forces your brain to continually retrieve different types of information, strengthening your ability to discriminate between concepts and apply knowledge flexibly.

4. Elaborative Interrogation: The Power of "Why?"

Elaborative interrogation involves asking "why" questions about the material you're studying. This technique, validated by research from the University of Memphis, helps you connect new information to existing knowledge.

While watching Crash Course videos, pause periodically to ask:

  • Why does this concept make sense?
  • Why would this process work this way?
  • Why is this information important?

The app's discussion prompts after each video are perfect for this technique. Don't just answer them literally—dig deeper into the underlying principles. This creates meaningful connections that make recall easier during exams.

5. Dual Coding: Combining Visual and Verbal Learning

Dual coding theory suggests that combining verbal and visual information creates two memory pathways instead of one. Research from the University of Waterloo shows this can improve retention by up to 40%.

Crash Course videos naturally employ dual coding with their animated explanations. To enhance this effect:

  • Pause videos to sketch diagrams of key concepts
  • Create mind maps connecting video content to your notes
  • Use the app's image-based flashcards whenever available
  • Explain concepts aloud while viewing relevant visuals

Implementing These Techniques with Crash Course

To integrate all five techniques into your Crash Course study routine:

  1. Start with active recall: Before a video, test what you already know
  2. Watch with dual coding: Take visual and verbal notes
  3. Use elaborative interrogation: Pause to ask "why" questions
  4. Practice interleaving: Mix subjects in each study session
  5. Apply spaced repetition: Use flashcards and quizzes regularly

A 2020 study tracking 1,200 students found that those combining these techniques improved their test scores by an average of 1.5 letter grades compared to traditional study methods.

The Science Behind the Success

These techniques work because they align with how human memory functions. Our brains evolved to remember:

  • Information recalled frequently (spaced repetition)
  • Knowledge tested in different contexts (interleaving)
  • Concepts connected to existing understanding (elaborative interrogation)
  • Experiences engaging multiple senses (dual coding)

By leveraging these natural memory mechanisms through the Crash Course app, you're not just studying harder—you're studying smarter. The app's features are specifically designed to facilitate these evidence-based techniques, making it easier than ever to implement them in your daily study routine.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even with these powerful techniques, students often undermine their own efforts through these common errors:

  • Passive watching: Just letting videos play without engagement
  • Over-reliance on one technique: Using only flashcards without video review
  • Inconsistent scheduling: Irregular study sessions that disrupt spaced repetition
  • Ignoring weak areas: Avoiding subjects you find difficult

Remember that effective learning often feels more challenging than passive review—that discomfort is a sign your brain is building new connections.

Measuring Your Progress

The Crash Course app tracks your quiz scores and flashcard performance, giving you concrete data on your improvement. Monitor these metrics to see which techniques work best for you:

  • Note score improvements after implementing a new technique
  • Track how quickly you recall information after spaced intervals
  • Compare performance between interleaved and blocked study sessions

This data-driven approach lets you refine your methods for maximum efficiency.

Conclusion: Building Better Learning Habits

Incorporating these five science-backed techniques into your Crash Course study routine can transform your learning efficiency. Start with one method, master it, then gradually add others. Within weeks, you'll notice significant improvements in:

  • Retention of complex information
  • Speed of recall during exams
  • Ability to connect concepts across subjects
  • Overall confidence in your knowledge

The best part? These techniques actually reduce total study time while improving results. By working with your brain's natural learning systems through the Crash Course app, you're setting yourself up for academic success that lasts far beyond your next exam.
