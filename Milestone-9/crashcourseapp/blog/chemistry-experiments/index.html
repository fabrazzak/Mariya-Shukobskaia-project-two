10 Safe Chemistry Experiments You Can Do at Home

Complement your Crash Course Chemistry learning with these fun, safe experiments using common household items.

June 15, 2025 • 7 min read

Hands-on experiments are one of the best ways to reinforce the concepts you're learning in our Crash Course Chemistry series. While professional labs have specialized equipment, many fundamental chemical principles can be demonstrated safely at home with common household items. These experiments will help bring your digital learning to life while keeping safety as the top priority.

Safety First: Essential Precautions

Before we dive into the experiments, let's review some crucial safety guidelines:

  • Always work in a well-ventilated area
  • Wear safety goggles if available (regular glasses can work in a pinch)
  • Keep a bowl of water or vinegar nearby for accidental spills
  • Never mix chemicals unless the experiment specifically calls for it
  • Work on a protected surface (newspaper or plastic tablecloth)
  • Have an adult present if you're under 16
  • Wash hands thoroughly after each experiment

Now that we've covered safety, let's explore 10 fascinating chemistry experiments you can do with items you probably already have at home.

1. The Classic Baking Soda and Vinegar Volcano

This timeless experiment demonstrates acid-base reactions, a fundamental concept in chemistry. When you mix baking soda (a base) with vinegar (an acid), they react to form carbon dioxide gas, water, and a salt called sodium acetate.

Materials needed: Baking soda, vinegar, a container, food coloring (optional)

Procedure: Place 2 tablespoons of baking soda in your container. Add a few drops of food coloring if desired. Pour in about ½ cup of vinegar and watch the reaction! The fizzing is the carbon dioxide gas being produced.

Chemistry connection: This demonstrates the reaction between acids and bases, which you'll explore in our Acid-Base Reactions episode. The chemical equation is:

NaHCO₃ + CH₃COOH → CO₂ + H₂O + CH₃COONa

2. Rainbow Milk Experiment (Surface Tension)

This colorful experiment demonstrates surface tension and the interaction between polar and nonpolar molecules.

Materials needed: Whole milk, food coloring, dish soap, cotton swabs, shallow dish

Procedure: Pour milk into the dish to cover the bottom. Add drops of different food coloring to the milk. Dip a cotton swab in dish soap, then touch it to the center of the milk. Watch the colors explode outward!

Chemistry connection: The soap molecules have a hydrophilic (water-loving) end and a hydrophobic (water-fearing) end. They race around trying to organize themselves with the fat molecules in the milk, creating the colorful movement. This relates to our Polar & Nonpolar Molecules episode.

3. DIY Lava Lamp (Density and Polarity)

Create your own mesmerizing lava lamp while learning about density and solubility.

Materials needed: Clear plastic bottle, water, vegetable oil, food coloring, Alka-Seltzer tablets

Procedure: Fill the bottle ¾ full with vegetable oil. Add water until nearly full, leaving about 2 inches at the top. Add 10 drops of food coloring. Break an Alka-Seltzer tablet into pieces and drop them in one at a time to see the "lava" effect.

Chemistry connection: Oil and water don't mix because water is polar and oil is nonpolar. The Alka-Seltzer reacts with water to produce carbon dioxide bubbles that carry colored water up through the oil. When the bubbles pop, the water sinks back down. This demonstrates concepts from our Solutions and Solubility episodes.

4. Invisible Ink (Oxidation Reactions)

Send secret messages while learning about oxidation-reduction reactions.

Materials needed: Lemon juice, water, cotton swab, white paper, heat source (light bulb or hairdryer)

Procedure: Mix equal parts lemon juice and water. Use a cotton swab to write a message on white paper. Let it dry completely. To reveal the message, gently heat the paper (hold it near a light bulb or use a hairdryer on low heat).

Chemistry connection: The weak acid in lemon juice oxidizes (combines with oxygen) faster than the paper when heated, turning brown first. This is a simple oxidation reaction similar to what we discuss in our Redox Reactions episode.

5. Homemade pH Indicator (Acids and Bases)

Create your own pH indicator from red cabbage to test household substances.

Materials needed: Red cabbage, boiling water, strainer, clear glasses, various household liquids to test (lemon juice, vinegar, baking soda solution, soap, etc.)

Procedure: Chop cabbage and soak in boiling water for 10 minutes. Strain out the cabbage, keeping the purple liquid. Pour small amounts into clear glasses and add different household liquids to test. The color will change based on pH!

Chemistry connection: Red cabbage contains anthocyanins, pigments that change color based on pH. Acids turn it pink/red, bases turn it green/yellow. This directly relates to our pH and pOH episode.

6. Dancing Raisins (Density and Gas Formation)

Watch raisins "dance" while learning about density and gas bubbles.

Materials needed: Clear glass, clear carbonated beverage (like sprite or club soda), raisins

Procedure: Fill the glass with the carbonated drink. Drop in a few raisins. Watch as they first sink, then rise to the surface, then sink again in a continuous dance.

Chemistry connection: The carbon dioxide bubbles attach to the rough surface of the raisins, decreasing their density and making them float. At the surface, the bubbles pop, increasing density so they sink again. This demonstrates gas solubility and density concepts from our Gases and Density episodes.

7. Egg in a Bottle (Gas Laws)

Demonstrate atmospheric pressure and gas laws with this classic experiment.

Materials needed: Hard-boiled egg, glass bottle with opening slightly smaller than egg, matches or lighter, adult supervision

Procedure: Peel the hard-boiled egg. Light a match and drop it into the bottle. Quickly place the egg on top. Watch as the egg gets sucked into the bottle!

Chemistry connection: The flame heats the air inside, which then cools rapidly, decreasing pressure inside the bottle. Higher outside atmospheric pressure pushes the egg in. This demonstrates Boyle's Law from our Gas Laws episode.

8. Magic Milk Plastic (Polymerization)

Turn milk into plastic while learning about polymers.

Materials needed: Milk, vinegar, measuring cups, strainer, paper towels, adult supervision for heating

Procedure: Heat 1 cup milk until steaming (not boiling). Add 4 teaspoons vinegar and stir. Strain out the curds and press between paper towels to dry. Mold into shapes and let harden overnight.

Chemistry connection: The acid causes the casein protein in milk to unfold and form long chains (polymerize), creating a natural plastic. This demonstrates polymerization concepts from our Polymers episode.

9. Fireproof Balloon (Heat Transfer)

Learn about heat capacity and heat transfer with this surprising experiment.

Materials needed: Balloons, water, candle, adult supervision

Procedure: Blow up a balloon and tie it. Fill another balloon with about ¼ cup water, then blow it up and tie it. Carefully hold the air-filled balloon over a candle flame - it pops immediately. Now try the water-filled balloon - it doesn't pop!

Chemistry connection: Water has a high heat capacity, absorbing the heat from the flame and preventing the rubber from getting hot enough to pop. This relates to our Thermochemistry episode.

10. Crystal Garden (Supersaturated Solutions)

Grow beautiful crystals while learning about supersaturated solutions.

Materials needed: Epsom salt, water, food coloring, jar, pipe cleaners (optional)

Procedure: Dissolve as much Epsom salt as possible in hot water (about 1.5 cups salt per cup water). Add food coloring if desired. Pour into jar. For shaped crystals, bend pipe cleaners into shapes and suspend in solution. Let sit undisturbed for several days as crystals form.

Chemistry connection: The hot water dissolves more salt than cold water can hold, creating a supersaturated solution. As it cools, the excess salt comes out of solution, forming crystals. This demonstrates solubility concepts from our Solutions episode.

Taking Your Chemistry Learning Further

These experiments are just the beginning! As you work through our Crash Course Chemistry series, try these extensions:

  • Keep a chemistry journal documenting your experiments, observations, and how they relate to course concepts
  • Vary one factor in each experiment (like using different acids in the volcano) and observe the changes
  • Research the chemical equations behind each reaction
  • Use our app's flashcard feature to quiz yourself on the concepts demonstrated
"The important thing in science is not so much to obtain new facts as to discover new ways of thinking about them." - William Lawrence Bragg

Remember, the goal isn't just to see cool reactions—it's to understand the chemistry behind them. Each time you complete an experiment, ask yourself:

  • What chemical principles does this demonstrate?
  • How does this relate to what I'm learning in Crash Course Chemistry?
  • What variables could I change to alter the outcome?
  • What real-world applications does this chemistry have?

By combining hands-on experimentation with our video lessons and app features, you'll develop a deeper, more practical understanding of chemistry that goes far beyond textbook learning.
