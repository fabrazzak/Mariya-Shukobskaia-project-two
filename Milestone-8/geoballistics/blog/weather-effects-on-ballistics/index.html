How Weather Affects Your Bullet's Path

GeoBallistics Team
12 min read

Weather conditions significantly impact bullet trajectory, often more than shooters realize. A 10°F temperature change or 20% humidity shift can alter your point of impact by several inches at 500 yards. This guide explores how atmospheric variables affect your shots and how GeoBallistics' advanced weather integration helps you compensate in real-time.

The Four Key Atmospheric Variables

When calculating ballistic solutions, four primary weather factors must be considered:

1. Temperature

Affects air density, powder burn rate, and barrel harmonics. Rule of thumb: 1°F change ≈ 1 MOA at 1000yds.

2. Barometric Pressure

Higher pressure increases air density. 1 inHg change ≈ 0.5 MOA at 1000yds.

3. Humidity

Counterintuitively, higher humidity decreases air density (water molecules displace heavier nitrogen/oxygen).

4. Altitude

Thinner air at higher elevations reduces drag. ~500ft elevation ≈ 0.1 mil difference at 1000yds.

Temperature Effects: Beyond Air Density

While most shooters understand temperature affects air density (colder = denser air = more drag), it actually influences bullet flight in three distinct ways:

1. Air Density Changes

The Ideal Gas Law (PV=nRT) shows that colder air is denser, creating more drag. This effect is non-linear:

Temp Change Effect at 500yds Effect at 1000yds
+20°F +0.2 mil +0.9 mil
-20°F -0.25 mil -1.1 mil

2. Powder Temperature Sensitivity

Colder powder burns slower, reducing muzzle velocity. Modern temp-stable powders mitigate but don't eliminate this effect:

  • Standard powders: ~1.5-2 fps/°F velocity change
  • Temp-stable powders: ~0.5-1 fps/°F

3. Barrel Harmonic Shifts

Metal contracts in cold, changing barrel vibration patterns. This can alter point of impact independent of trajectory changes.

Pressure & Altitude: The Hidden Variables

Barometric pressure and altitude are often misunderstood or mismeasured by shooters:

Station Pressure vs. Barometric Pressure

Weather reports give sea-level adjusted pressure, but bullets experience local station pressure. GeoBallistics automatically:

  • Converts reported pressure using your GPS altitude
  • Or uses direct station pressure from connected devices

The Density Altitude Concept

Density altitude combines temperature, pressure, and humidity into a single equivalent altitude. Key points:

  • Hot, humid, low-pressure days act like higher altitude
  • Cold, dry, high-pressure days act like lower altitude
  • GeoBallistics PRO calculates density altitude in real-time

Field Observation:

At 1000 yards, a 2000ft density altitude increase (e.g., from 70°F to 90°F at sea level) reduces drop by approximately 0.3 mils for .308 Winchester.

Humidity: The Counterintuitive Factor

Unlike most substances, water vapor is less dense than dry air (H₂O molecular weight = 18 vs. N₂ = 28 and O₂ = 32). Therefore:

High Humidity

  • Water molecules displace heavier nitrogen/oxygen
  • Air becomes less dense
  • Bullet experiences less drag
  • Point of impact rises slightly

Low Humidity

  • More nitrogen/oxygen molecules present
  • Air becomes more dense
  • Bullet experiences more drag
  • Point of impact drops slightly

While humidity's effect is smaller than temperature or pressure, it becomes significant beyond 600 yards. A 50% humidity change ≈ 0.1 mil at 1000yds.

Practical Compensation Strategies

When you can't access real-time weather data, these field techniques help:

1. The 1% Rule

For every 10°F change from your zero conditions, expect ~1% change in elevation at long range.

2. Morning vs. Afternoon Shift

If zeroed at 70°F morning, anticipate shooting lower in 90°F afternoon by ~0.2 mils at 800yds.

3. Seasonal Dope Book

Maintain separate data cards for summer/winter conditions with pre-calculated adjustments.
