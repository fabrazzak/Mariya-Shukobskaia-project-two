What's the Difference Between Light, Medium, and Dark Roast Coffee?

Understanding how roast levels affect flavor, caffeine content, and brewing methods

If you've ever stood in front of a shelf full of coffee bags wondering what "light," "medium," or "dark roast" actually means, you're not alone. These terms describe how long and at what temperature the coffee beans have been roasted, which dramatically affects their flavor profile, caffeine content, and best brewing methods. At Sumato Coffee, we believe understanding these differences can help you find your perfect cup.

The Roasting Process Explained

Coffee roasting transforms green coffee beans into the fragrant brown beans we know and love. During roasting, beans undergo chemical changes as they're rapidly brought to very high temperatures. When they reach the peak of perfection, they're quickly cooled to stop the process. The entire roasting process typically takes between 10-20 minutes for most commercial roasters, with the exact time depending on the desired roast level.

As beans roast, they go through several stages:

  • Drying phase: Beans lose moisture and turn from green to yellow
  • First crack: A popping sound occurs as sugars caramelize and beans expand
  • Development phase: Flavor compounds develop as roasting continues
  • Second crack: A sharper cracking sound as oils emerge (for darker roasts)

Light Roast Coffee

Light roast coffee is roasted for the shortest time, typically stopping just after the first crack (around 350-400°F). These beans reach an internal temperature of about 356-401°F. Light roasts have a light brown color, no oil on the surface, and retain most of the coffee's original characteristics from its origin.

Characteristics:

  • Flavor: Bright acidity, pronounced fruity or floral notes, more complex flavors that reflect the bean's origin
  • Body: Lighter body, more tea-like
  • Caffeine content: Slightly higher (by volume) than darker roasts
  • Best for: Pour over, drip, and other methods that highlight subtle flavors

Light roasts are sometimes called "cinnamon roast" (not because of flavor but because of the light brown color) or "half city roast." Our Sender blend is a great example of a well-balanced light roast with citrusy brightness and honey-like sweetness.

"Light roast doesn't mean weak coffee - it means coffee where you can actually taste the bean's origin characteristics rather than just roast flavors." - Ben Martinez, Sumato Founder

Medium Roast Coffee

Medium roast coffee reaches internal temperatures between 410-428°F, stopping somewhere between the end of the first crack and just before the second crack begins. This is the most popular roast level in American specialty coffee shops.

Characteristics:

  • Flavor: Balanced acidity and body, caramel sweetness, some origin character with added roast flavors
  • Body: Medium body, rounded mouthfeel
  • Caffeine content: Middle ground between light and dark
  • Best for: Nearly all brewing methods, especially drip coffee and French press

Medium roasts are sometimes called "American roast," "breakfast roast," or "city roast." Our Touch Base blend exemplifies a perfect medium roast with its notes of milk chocolate and toasted nuts.

Dark Roast Coffee

Dark roast coffee continues roasting past the second crack, reaching internal temperatures of 437-446°F or higher. The beans become shiny as oils emerge on the surface, and they take on a dark brown, almost black color.

Characteristics:

  • Flavor: Bold, smoky, sometimes bitter, with pronounced roast flavors dominating origin characteristics
  • Body: Full, heavy body
  • Caffeine content: Slightly less than lighter roasts (by volume)
  • Best for: Espresso, cold brew, and those who prefer bold, intense flavors

Common names for dark roasts include "French roast," "Italian roast," or "espresso roast." Our Rise & Grinder is a popular dark roast with deep chocolate notes and a smooth finish.

Caffeine Content Myths

There's a common misconception that dark roast has more caffeine than light roast. The truth is more nuanced:

  • By volume: Light roast has slightly more caffeine because the beans are denser (you get more coffee per scoop)
  • By weight: They're nearly identical because caffeine is relatively stable during roasting
  • In practice: The difference is minimal - about a 5-10% variation at most

Choosing Your Perfect Roast

The "best" roast level is entirely subjective and depends on your personal taste preferences. Here's how to decide:

You might prefer light roast if:

  • You enjoy tasting the unique characteristics of different coffee origins
  • You appreciate bright, acidic flavors like citrus or berries
  • You typically drink coffee black
  • You use pour over or drip brewing methods

You might prefer medium roast if:

  • You want balance between origin flavors and roast characteristics
  • You enjoy notes of caramel, nuts, or milk chocolate
  • You drink coffee with or without milk
  • You use a variety of brewing methods

You might prefer dark roast if:

  • You love bold, intense coffee flavors
  • You prefer lower acidity
  • You typically add milk or cream
  • You make espresso or cold brew

At Sumato, we recommend trying all three roast levels to discover your preference. Our Coffee Collection sampler lets you taste test multiple roasts side by side.

Brewing Tips for Each Roast Level

To get the best flavor from each roast type, consider these brewing recommendations:

Light Roast:

  • Use slightly hotter water (205°F) to fully extract flavors
  • Try a longer brew time (4+ minutes for French press)
  • Use a finer grind than you would for darker roasts
  • Best methods: Pour over, Aeropress, siphon

Medium Roast:

  • Standard brewing parameters work well (200°F water, medium grind)
  • Very versatile - adapts well to most methods
  • Excellent for automatic drip machines
  • Best methods: Drip, French press, Aeropress, pour over

Dark Roast:

  • Use slightly cooler water (195°F) to avoid over-extraction
  • Shorter brew times to prevent bitterness
  • Coarser grind helps balance the intensity
  • Best methods: Espresso, moka pot, cold brew, French press

Remember that coffee freshness matters just as much as roast level. All our Sumato coffees are roasted to order and stamped with the roast date so you always know you're getting the freshest possible beans.

The Sumato Approach to Roasting

At our Utah roastery, we take a tailored approach to each coffee we roast. Rather than forcing all beans into predetermined roast profiles, we cup test each lot to determine its ideal roast level. Some beans shine as light roasts, others develop beautifully at medium, and a few can handle dark roasting while maintaining complexity.

Our roasting team monitors time, temperature, and color development carefully, making adjustments based on the bean's moisture content, density, and origin characteristics. We never roast darker just to cover up inferior beans - when you see a dark roast from Sumato, it's because that coffee tastes its best at that roast level.
